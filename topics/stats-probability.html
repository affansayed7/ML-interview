Statistics &
Probability Primer

Every ML algorithm downstream — regression, Naive Bayes, evaluation metrics — is really applied statistics. This is the vocabulary interviewers assume you already have.

1. Mean, variance & standard deviation

The mean is the center of mass of your data. Variance measures how spread out the data is from that center — but it's in squared units, which is awkward to interpret, so we take its square root to get the standard deviation, which is back in the original units.

Population mean
$$ \mu = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N} x_i $$
Variance
$$ \sigma^2 = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N} (x_i - \mu)^2 $$
Standard deviation
$$ \sigma = \sqrt{\sigma^2} $$
Population vs sample When you compute variance from a sample rather than the full population, divide by $n-1$ instead of $n$ (Bessel's correction). This corrects a bias: a sample's own mean is closer to its points than the true population mean is, so dividing by $n$ would systematically underestimate the true variance.

2. Probability basics

Two events are independent if the outcome of one tells you nothing about the other: $P(A \cap B) = P(A)P(B)$. They're dependent otherwise — knowing $A$ happened changes your belief about $B$.

Conditional probability — the probability of $A$ given that $B$ has already happened — is written $P(A|B)$, and is the foundation for the next section.

Conditional probability
$$ P(A|B) = \frac{P(A \cap B)}{P(B)} \quad \text{provided } P(B) \neq 0$$

3. Bayes' theorem

Bayes' theorem lets you flip a conditional probability around — go from $P(B|A)$ (easy to measure) to $P(A|B)$ (what you actually want to know). It's derived directly from the conditional probability formula above, applied in both directions.

Bayes' theorem
$$ P(A|B) = \frac{P(B|A)\,P(A)}{P(B)} $$
Worked example — the classic interview trap A disease affects 1% of a population. A test is 99% accurate (for both sick and healthy people). You test positive — what's the actual probability you're sick? Intuition says ~99%. Bayes says otherwise: out of 10,000 people, ~100 are sick (99 correctly flagged) and ~9,900 are healthy (~99 incorrectly flagged). So $P(\text{sick}|\text{positive}) = 99/(99+99) \approx 50\%$ — not 99%. This is why base rates matter, and it's a favorite interview follow-up.

4. Common distributions

A Bernoulli distribution models a single yes/no trial (coin flip). A Binomial distribution models the count of successes across $n$ independent Bernoulli trials. The Normal (Gaussian) distribution is the bell curve — and it shows up everywhere in ML because of the Central Limit Theorem below.

Normal distribution PDF
$$ f(x) = \frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}} \, e^{-\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{x-\mu}{\sigma}\right)^2} $$

5. Central Limit Theorem

If you repeatedly sample from any distribution (even a skewed one) and compute the mean of each sample, the distribution of those sample means approaches a normal distribution as sample size grows — regardless of the shape of the original distribution. This is why so many statistical methods (confidence intervals, hypothesis tests, even some ML assumptions) lean on normality even when raw data isn't normal.

6. Correlation vs causation

Correlation measures how two variables move together, typically via the Pearson correlation coefficient (ranges from −1 to 1). It says nothing about why — two variables can move together because one causes the other, because a third hidden variable causes both (a confounder), or by pure coincidence.

Pearson correlation coefficient
$$ r = \frac{\sum (x_i - \bar{x})(y_i - \bar{y})}{\sqrt{\sum(x_i-\bar{x})^2}\sqrt{\sum(y_i-\bar{y})^2}} $$

Interview Q&A

Why do we divide by $n-1$ instead of $n$ when computing sample variance?

Because the sample mean is computed from the same data, it's slightly closer to the sample points than the true population mean would be — this makes the raw variance estimate biased (too small). Dividing by $n-1$ instead of $n$ (Bessel's correction) corrects that bias, giving an unbiased estimator of the true population variance.

Likely follow-up "What happens to this correction as $n$ gets large?" — the difference between dividing by $n$ and $n-1$ shrinks to negligible as sample size grows.
Explain Bayes' theorem in plain terms, and where does it show up in ML?

It's a rule for updating a belief (prior) once you see new evidence, producing an updated belief (posterior), weighted by how likely that evidence is under your hypothesis versus overall. In ML it's the entire basis of the Naive Bayes classifier, and it underlies Bayesian approaches to hyperparameter tuning and probabilistic modeling in general.

Likely follow-up "Walk me through the disease-testing example" — be ready to show that even a 99%-accurate test can mean less than 50% real probability of disease when the base rate (prior) is low.
What is the Central Limit Theorem, and why does it matter for ML?

It states that the sampling distribution of the mean of a large enough number of independent samples approaches a normal distribution, regardless of the original data's distribution. It matters because it justifies using normal-distribution-based statistics (confidence intervals, significance tests, certain error assumptions) even when your raw underlying data isn't normally distributed.

Likely follow-up "Does CLT require the original data to be normal?" — No, that's the entire point: it works for (almost) any underlying distribution, given a large enough sample size.
Correlation does not imply causation — give a concrete example and explain why.

Ice cream sales and drowning deaths are positively correlated, but ice cream doesn't cause drowning — both are driven by a confounding variable: hot weather increases both. Correlation only measures co-movement, not the causal mechanism, so acting on a correlation as if it were causal can lead to wrong conclusions or wasted interventions.

Likely follow-up "How would you actually establish causation?" — controlled experiments/A-B testing, or causal inference techniques like instrumental variables when experiments aren't possible.
What's the difference between a probability and a likelihood?

Probability asks: given fixed parameters, how likely is this data? Likelihood asks the reverse: given this observed data, how plausible are different parameter values? This distinction is exactly why Maximum Likelihood Estimation (used to derive loss functions like log-loss in Logistic Regression) is framed the way it is — you fix the data and search over parameters.

Likely follow-up "Where will you see Maximum Likelihood Estimation again later in this syllabus?" — Logistic Regression's log-loss is derived directly from MLE, and you'll see it again in §4.
